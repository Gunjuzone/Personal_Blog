ON RESPONSIBILITIES & BLACK TAX

Dear Friends,

How often do we think about the weight we carry, the invisible threads that tether us to others, weaving our lives into a tapestry of shared responsibilities? I’ve been sitting with this thought lately, grappling with the reality of what it means to be the person others rely on, the one who feels the pull of duty like an ever-present undercurrent, subtle but unrelenting. For some of us, these responsibilities are heavier, more intricate, bound by culture, history, and an unspoken understanding of what it means to succeed - not just for ourselves, but for everyone tied to us. This is the essence of what many call black tax.

If you’re not familiar, black tax isn’t written down anywhere. It isn’t a contract you sign or an agreement you formally enter. It’s more of a cultural inheritance - a rite of passage embedded in love, duty, and survival. It’s the expectation - spoken or not - that as you climb, you pull others up with you. It’s remitting part of your paycheck to the family back home, funding a sibling’s education, or making sure that the lights stay on in a household you no longer live in. It’s love, duty, pride, gratitude - and sometimes, a quiet resentment - all tangled up in a single package.

Lately, I’ve been reflecting on the duality of this responsibility. On the one hand, it’s a privilege to be in a position where you can give back, to honor the sacrifices that brought you to where you are. It’s an act of love, a way of saying, “I see you, and I’m grateful for what you’ve done for me.” But on the other hand, the weight can be suffocating. There’s an unspoken pressure to keep going, to succeed at all costs, because failure doesn’t just affect you - it ripples outward, touching everyone who depends on you. And on those days, I find myself asking: Whose dreams am I chasing? Are they mine, or are they shaped by the expectations of others?

It’s a hard question to sit with. There’s guilt in even asking it. How can I feel overwhelmed when I’ve been given opportunities others only dream of? How can I resent what is, at its heart, an act of love?

But I am learning that it’s okay to feel conflicted. It’s okay to carry this weight and still feel the strain. It’s okay to want to give back while also wanting to hold back. The key, I think, lies in balance - learning to honor our responsibilities without losing ourselves in them. It’s about setting boundaries, even when it feels selfish, and remembering that you can’t pour from an empty cup. It’s about acknowledging that while we owe much to our loved ones, we also owe something to ourselves.

So, Friend, if you’re carrying this weight too, I hope you’ll give yourself permission to breathe. To trust that you’re doing enough, even when it feels like you aren’t. To know that your worth isn’t tied to what you can provide for others. You’re allowed to rest, to dream, to pursue a life that fulfills you - not just the people around you.

Thank you for listening. I wish you clarity, strength, and the courage to honor both your responsibilities and your dreams. You’ve got this.

Shakur
